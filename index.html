Project Overview

This project explores the design and prototyping of a Direct Methanol Fuel Cell (DMFC) system using a 10-cell Flex-Stak from the Fuel Cell Store. The cell operates on 3% methanol solution (~1M), and is designed to produce sufficient voltage and current to charge a 20,000 mAh battery pack within 24 hours.

Our prototype integrates fluid dynamics (via peristaltic pumping), real-time monitoring (via Arduino and sensors), and electrical circuitry for power conditioning. We aim to validate theoretical predictions of fuel consumption, current density (~50‚Äì100 mA/cm¬≤), and overall efficiency (~20‚Äì40%).

Key design constraints include minimizing methanol crossover, maximizing fuel cell power output, and designing a dynamic circuitry for live readings and feedback.

How the DMFC Works

In a DMFC, methanol (CH3OH) is oxidized at the anode:

Protons pass through a proton exchange membrane (PEM) to the cathode, while electrons go through an external circuit. At the cathode:

(3/2)O2 + 6H+ + 6e‚àí ‚Üí 3H2O

The theoretical cell voltage is ~1.20 V, but practical output is closer to 0.3‚Äì0.6 V due to real-world losses.

How to Build and Run the Setup

Materials Used

  • 10-cell Flex-Stak DMFC
  • Peristaltic pump, tubing, check valves
  • Arduino Uno + sensors (voltage, current, flow)
  • 1M MeOH-water solution (3% by weight)
  • Custom control circuit with MOSFETs

Setup Instructions

  1. Assemble and torque stack (8‚Äì10 in-lbs)
  2. Hydrate with DI water before feeding MeOH
  3. Start MeOH flow at 100 cc/min
  4. Connect sensors and power electronics
  5. Begin Arduino logging and output analysis
